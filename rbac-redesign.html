CQPortal RBAC Redesign

Affiliate role-based access control redesign — Epic #300 and issues #301–305

Design Documentation Camp Quest

Welcome

This document is the complete design specification for the CQPortal affiliate RBAC redesign. It covers the full pipeline — from the permission catalog and data models through migration strategy, default templates, the access-check refactor, and the team-facing UI for managing groups and permissions.

The redesign replaces the flat two-role affiliate model (AFFILIATE_ADMIN and AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER) with a granular UserGroup → PermissionSet → Permission hierarchy, enabling least-privilege access scoped to real-world camp operations.

Affiliate RBAC Redesign

UserGroups, PermissionSets, and granular permissions

Epic #300 enhancement

Why This Redesign

The current system has only 2 affiliate roles: AFFILIATE_ADMIN and AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER. Originally there were 4 roles, but migration 0036 collapsed AFFILIATE_REGISTRAR and AFFILIATE_MEDICAL_STAFF because the flat model couldn't express real-world org charts. Two roles cannot capture the nuance of camp operations where staff wear different hats and need scoped, least-privilege access.

Real-world consequences of the flat role model
  • A camp director who needs cabin assignments must be given AFFILIATE_ADMIN, which also grants community moderation, library deletion, and full reports — violating least privilege.
  • A registrar who needs to see housing assignments to coordinate roommates has no read-only option — it's full admin or nothing.
  • A camp nurse who needs health records and rosters gets AFFILIATE_ADMIN as the only path, exposing them to financial data, BGC status, and volunteer management they shouldn't see.
  • Volunteers who wear multiple hats (camp director + board president) can't cleanly separate operational access from board governance.

The Team & Roles UI at /o/<org>/a/<slug>/team/ will be evolved, not replaced. The redesign layers a flexible permission model on top of the existing affiliate infrastructure rather than introducing a parallel system.

User Stories

The flat two-role model forces over-granting or under-granting access. These scenarios show how the new granular permission model maps permissions to real duties — without creating a new role string for every combination.

Committee Member vs. Committee Head

A committee head needs to see who applied, approve applications, and manage the committee roster. A regular committee member only needs to view the volunteer list and read session details — they should not see BGC status or approve applications.

Duty Committee Member Committee Head
View volunteer list volunteers.read volunteers.read
Approve volunteer applications — volunteers.approve
Manage background checks — volunteers.bgc_manage
View session rosters sessions.read sessions.read
Assign volunteers to sessions — volunteers.write
Why this matters

Under the flat model, both users get AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER or neither gets access at all. The committee member either sees BGC status and can approve applications (over-granted) or can't see the volunteer list at all (under-granted).

Staffing Committee: Lead, BGC Manager, Interviewer, Member

A staffing committee handles volunteer onboarding for an affiliate. The committee has a lead who oversees the whole process, a designated member who manages background checks, a pool of interviewers who conduct applicant interviews, and general committee members who help with logistics. Each role needs different access to do their job — and only their job.

The committee structure:

  • Staffing Lead — runs the committee, approves applications, assigns interviewers, sees everything. Also conducts interviews.
  • BGC Manager — a committee member tasked with background checks; sees BGC status but cannot approve applications
  • Interviewer — conducts applicant interviews; sees who they're interviewing and schedules slots, but cannot see BGC status or approve/reject
  • Committee Member — helps with logistics; sees the volunteer list and session rosters, nothing sensitive
Duty Committee Member Interviewer BGC Manager Staffing Lead
View volunteer list volunteers.read volunteers.read volunteers.read volunteers.read
View session rosters sessions.read sessions.read sessions.read sessions.read
Manage background checks — — volunteers.bgc_manage volunteers.bgc_manage
Approve / reject applications — — — volunteers.approve
Assign volunteers to sessions — — — volunteers.write
Schedule interviews — events.write — events.write
View compliance reports — — — reports.view_volunteer
How the new model handles this

Four UserGroups, four PermissionSets — no new role strings:

  • Staffing Lead UserGroup → volunteers.read + volunteers.bgc_manage + volunteers.approve + volunteers.write + events.write + reports.view_volunteer (lead is also a member of Interviewers for events.write)
  • BGC Manager UserGroup → volunteers.read + volunteers.bgc_manage
  • Interviewers UserGroup → volunteers.read + events.write
  • Staffing Committee UserGroup → volunteers.read + sessions.read

Under the flat model, all four would need AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER to see the volunteer list — which also gives every one of them BGC access and approval power. Or none get access and the committee can't function.

Overlapping membership

A user can belong to multiple UserGroups. The staffing lead who also conducts interviews is in both the Staffing Lead and Interviewers groups — their effective permissions are the union of both sets. The lead gets volunteers.approve, volunteers.bgc_manage, and events.write without anyone needing to create a compound role string like AFFILIATE_STAFFING_LEAD_INTERVIEWER. Same pattern applies if the BGC manager also wants to help with interviews — just add them to the Interviewers group; no other changes needed.

Camp Director Who Is Also Board President

A volunteer serves as both camp director for an affiliate and president of the board. Operational access (housing, sessions, rosters) and governance access (board resolutions, voting, COI disclosures) are entirely separate concerns.

Capability Source Scope
Housing, sessions, events, staff management UserGroup Camp Director Affiliate-scoped (operational)
Board resolutions, voting, COI BoardMember (unchanged) Governance (separate system)
Why this matters

Board governance stays separate from the permission model by design (Design Principle #4). The camp director's operational permissions are cleanly contained within the affiliate scope. Board access comes from BoardMember, not from any UserGroup or PermissionSet. The two never interfere.

Camp Nurse with Read-Only Roster Access

A camp nurse needs health records and session rosters but should not see financial data, BGC status, or volunteer management. Under the flat model, AFFILIATE_ADMIN is the only path to rosters — it also grants everything else.

Duty Camp Nurse
View health records medical.*
View session rosters sessions.read
View attendance reports reports.view_attendance
Financial data, BGC status, volunteer management — (not granted)
Cabin Lead: Session-Scoped Access to Camper Medical Records

At Camp Quest NorthWest, cabin counselors are handed physical copies of their campers' medical records during registration check-in. This is a manual, error-prone process — records get lost, the wrong packet goes to the wrong cabin, and there's no audit trail. CQPortal should let a cabin lead see medical records for the campers assigned to their cabins for a specific session — and only their cabins, only that session.

Duty Cabin Staff Cabin Lead Housing Coordinator
View own cabin assignments housing.read (scoped) housing.read (scoped) housing.* (all)
View camper rosters for own cabin sessions.read (scoped) sessions.read (scoped) sessions.read (all)
View medical records for campers in own cabin medical.read (cabin-scoped) medical.read (cabin-scoped) —
View medical records for all campers in session — — — (Medical Staff only)
Reassign campers between cabins — — housing.write
Session and cabin scoping

The permission catalog currently has flat permissions like medical.read. This story requires a scoping mechanism that filters records by the user's cabin assignment for the active session. The permission check would be: medical.read AND the camper is in a cabin assigned to this user for this session. This is a new capability — the current model grants medical.read globally or not at all.

Sr Staff: Camp Session Roles

Sr Staff are volunteers who take on elevated roles for a specific camp session — not year-round. Someone might be a cabin lead for Session A but just a regular counselor for Session B. Or a program lead for one session and a regular volunteer at the next. These roles are session-scoped: the access exists for the duration of that session and expires when the session ends.

Duty Counselor Cabin Lead Program Lead
View session roster sessions.read (session-scoped) sessions.read (session-scoped) sessions.read (session-scoped)
View camper medical records for own cabin medical.read (cabin-scoped) medical.read (cabin-scoped) —
Manage cabin assignments for own cabin group — housing.write (cabin-group-scoped) —
Run check-in for assigned session — — registrations.write (session-scoped)
Assign volunteers to activities — — events.write (session-scoped)
How teams handle this

A session-scoped team (e.g. "Session A — Cabin Leads") grants group memberships that are valid only for that session. When the session ends, the team's memberships expire automatically. The cabin lead for Session A is added to the session-scoped team; for Session B they're in a different team (or no team). No permanent role changes needed — the team membership carries the access and the expiration.

Session-scoped permissions: not yet in the catalog

The permission catalog and the PermissionSet model currently have no concept of scoping — a permission is granted or it isn't, with no session or cabin filter. Session-scoped access requires a new model layer that links a UserGroup membership to a specific Session (or set of cabins within a session) and an expiration date. This is a design question for the implementation phase: do we scope at the group level (a group is session-scoped) or at the membership level (a user's membership in a group is session-scoped)? The user stories above assume the latter — a membership can be time-limited and cabin-filtered.

Interview scheduling: not yet a feature

The scenarios above reference interview scheduling (events.write) as if it exists today. It does not — CQPortal has no dedicated interview scheduling module yet. These user stories assume the permission catalog will be extended when that feature is built. The events.write permission is used here as a placeholder for what an interviews.schedule permission would look like once the feature exists. Adding it to the catalog later requires only a new Python constant and a one-line entry in the catalog dict — no migration, no role string, no UI change.

Architecture

The redesign introduces a three-tier model that maps users to granular permissions through affiliate-scoped groups and named permission sets.

What Stays the Same

The redesign is additive. The following roles and structures remain as RoleAssignment and are untouched by the new model:

  • PARENT — stays as RoleAssignment
  • VOLUNTEER — stays as RoleAssignment
  • PLATFORM_ADMIN — stays as RoleAssignment
  • CENTRAL_* roles — stay as RoleAssignment
  • Board governance (BoardMember) — stays separate from the permission system

Design Principles

1. Restricted by default

Affiliates expand permissions, not contract. The core template starts narrow; affiliates add groups or permission sets to grant more access, never to remove it.

2. Python-defined permissions

Permissions ship with code, not DB-configurable. This keeps the catalog under version control and subject to code review, preventing accidental privilege escalation through a database edit.

3. Unified UI

Evolve the existing Team & Roles page, no parallel system. The affiliate staff management experience stays in one place; the new group/permission model is surfaced through the page affiliates already use.

4. Board governance stays separate

BoardMember is unchanged. Board access is a governance relationship, not an operational permission, so it remains orthogonal to the permission model.

5. Migration guarantee

No access lost, no access gained. Every existing AFFILIATE_ADMIN keeps full access; every AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER keeps their volunteer management access. The migration is a re-expression of current state, not a re-granting.

6. Org isolation enforced at model level

The chain GroupMembership → UserGroup → Affiliate → Organization means a permission check can never silently cross an org boundary. Isolation is structural, not a convention.

Security Considerations
  • Permissions are Python-defined, not database-driven — prevents misconfiguration granting unintended access. A database edit can never create a new permission or widen an existing one.
  • Org isolation enforced at model level — GroupMembership → UserGroup → Affiliate → Organization. The permission check traversal can never cross an org boundary.
  • Self-lockout prevention — you cannot remove your own last group with staff.manage. The system blocks the action before it commits.
  • Central role escalation preserved — CENTRAL_SUPERADMIN passes all checks in the same org, so central staff retain their override capability.
  • No access lost or gained during migration — the migration is a faithful re-expression of the current role assignments into the new group/permission model.

Permission Catalog

Granular capability registry — the foundation of the RBAC redesign

#301 enhancement depends on: none

The Problem

Currently access control is binary — you hold a role string (AFFILIATE_ADMIN) or you don't. There is no way to express "can read housing but not write" or "can approve volunteers but not manage BGC" without adding a new role for every combination. The result is either over-granted access (give full admin because a narrow capability is needed) or under-granted access (no path exists at all). The flat role model cannot compose.

The Solution

A Python-defined permission registry in apps/tenancy/permissions.py. Permissions are constants grouped by domain. The registry is the single source of truth: views and decorators check against it, and new permissions require code review and a PR.

python 
# apps/tenancy/permissions.py

HOUSING_READ      = "housing.read"
HOUSING_WRITE     = "housing.write"
HOUSING_AUTO_ASSIGN = "housing.auto_assign"

PERMISSION_CATALOG = {
    "housing": {
        HOUSING_READ: "View cabin assignments and housing board",
        HOUSING_WRITE: "Create, edit, and delete cabin assignments",
        HOUSING_AUTO_ASSIGN: "Run automatic cabin assignment algorithm",
    },
    # ... more domains
}

Every permission is a domain.action string. Domains are the top-level grouping (housing, volunteers, registrations, etc.) and actions are the granular capability within that domain (.read, .write, .auto_assign). The catalog maps each permission constant to a human-readable description so the UI and audit logs can show what a permission actually does.

Design Decisions

1. Python-defined, not database-driven

Permissions ship with code releases, not as DB rows. This is safer — a misconfigured database entry can never grant a capability that wasn't already reviewed in a PR. The catalog lives in version control, diffed and discussed like any other code.

2. Domain-prefixed naming

Every permission follows the domain.action format. This makes permissions grep-able: a search for housing. finds every housing capability across the codebase, and a search for .read finds every read permission. The prefix is the domain, the suffix is the action.

3. Read/write separation

Every domain that has writable state exposes both .read and .write. This is the minimum viable separation: a role can always be granted read-only access to a domain without write. Additional manage-tier actions (like .auto_assign or .delete) layer on top of the read/write baseline.

4. Board governance excluded

Board access is a governance relationship modeled by BoardMember, not an operational permission. Board members don't get staff permissions; they get board-specific capabilities through a separate model. Keeping the catalog focused on operational access prevents the two concerns from leaking into each other.

Questions for the Community

We want your input on the catalog
  • Are there any permissions missing from the catalog?
  • Is the domain grouping intuitive, or should domains be reorganized?
  • Should medical.write be split into medical.write_records and medical.write_emergency?
  • Are there affiliate-specific permissions that should be in the core catalog?

These questions are open. The catalog is the foundation every later issue builds on, so getting the permission vocabulary right now saves rework later. If a capability is missing or a domain boundary feels wrong, now is the time to say so.

Security Considerations
  • Permissions ship with code releases, not DB-configurable. A database edit cannot create or widen a permission — only a code change, reviewed and merged, can.
  • A misconfigured DB permission could grant unintended access — this is why the catalog is Python-defined. The database stores assignments (who has which permission set), not definitions (what a permission does).
  • New permissions require code review and PR approval. Adding a permission means adding a constant to permissions.py, which is visible in the diff and subject to the same scrutiny as any access-control change.
  • The catalog is the single source of truth. Views check against it; a permission string that isn't in the catalog is rejected, not silently accepted.

Models & Migration

UserGroup, PermissionSet, GroupMembership + RoleAssignment data migration

#302 enhancement depends on: #301

The Problem

RoleAssignment stores a flat role string per user per affiliate/org. There is no concept of groups, no granular permissions, and no read vs. write distinction. A user is either an AFFILIATE_ADMIN or an AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER, and the decorator @require_affiliate_role grants all-or-nothing access based on that single string.

This makes it impossible to grant someone read-only access to housing, or write access to volunteer management without also giving them full admin. Every new staff function requires a new role constant, a new migration, and a new decorator branch.

The Three New Models

Three new Django models replace the flat role string with a layered, org-isolated structure:

python 
from django.db import models
from django.conf import settings

class PermissionSet(models.Model):
    """A named set of granular permissions, scoped to an organization."""
    name        = models.CharField(max_length=100)
    affiliate   = models.ForeignKey('tenancy.Affiliate', null=True, blank=True, on_delete=models.CASCADE)
    organization = models.ForeignKey('tenancy.Organization', on_delete=models.CASCADE)
    permissions = models.JSONField(default=list)  # e.g. ['housing.read', 'housing.write', ...]
    is_system   = models.BooleanField(default=False)  # system defaults can't be deleted
    description = models.TextField(blank=True, default='')

    class Meta:
        unique_together = [('organization', 'name')]

class UserGroup(models.Model):
    """A named group within an affiliate, linked to a permission set."""
    name          = models.CharField(max_length=100)
    affiliate     = models.ForeignKey('tenancy.Affiliate', on_delete=models.CASCADE)
    permission_set = models.ForeignKey(PermissionSet, on_delete=models.PROTECT)
    description   = models.TextField(blank=True, default='')

    class Meta:
        unique_together = [('affiliate', 'name')]

class GroupMembership(models.Model):
    """A user's membership in a group, with audit trail."""
    user       = models.ForeignKey(settings.AUTH_USER_MODEL, on_delete=models.CASCADE)
    group      = models.ForeignKey(UserGroup, on_delete=models.CASCADE)
    assigned_by = models.ForeignKey(settings.AUTH_USER_MODEL, on_delete=models.SET_NULL, null=True, related_name='memberships_assigned')

    class Meta:
        unique_together = [('user', 'group')]

Before / After Comparison

Before — RoleAssignment
  • Flat role string per user per affiliate
  • Two roles: AFFILIATE_ADMIN, AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER
  • All-or-nothing access via @require_affiliate_role
  • No read/write distinction
  • Previously had AFFILIATE_REGISTRAR and AFFILIATE_MEDICAL_STAFF (collapsed in migration 0036)
After — Groups & PermissionSets
  • Users in multiple groups, each with a permission set
  • Granular permissions: housing.read, housing.write, volunteers.approve, etc.
  • Read/write separation per domain
  • Affiliates customize groups and permission sets
  • System defaults auto-provisioned on affiliate creation

Data Migration

The migration runs in two phases to guarantee zero access changes:

Phase 1 — Provision Defaults

For each existing affiliate, create the default permission sets and groups (see #303). This is idempotent — re-running will not duplicate existing sets or groups.

Phase 2 — Map Existing Assignments

Map existing RoleAssignment rows to new group memberships:

Old Role New Group New Permission Set
AFFILIATE_ADMIN Affiliate Admin Affiliate Admin (restricted oversight)
AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER Volunteer Coordinator Volunteer Coordinator

A user with both roles gets memberships in both groups. Central role escalation works from day one — CENTRAL_SUPERADMIN passes all permission checks in the same org without needing a group membership.

Migration Guarantee

No access lost, no access gained. Effective permissions match pre-migration access exactly. Old RoleAssignment rows are preserved for rollback safety.

What Stays as RoleAssignment

Not all roles migrate to the new group/permission model. The following remain on RoleAssignment:

  • PARENT and VOLUNTEER — end-user roles, not staff functions
  • PLATFORM_ADMIN — global, not affiliate-scoped
  • CENTRAL_SUPERADMIN, CENTRAL_LIBRARY_ADMIN, CENTRAL_REGISTRAR — org-wide, only one org, can migrate later

Terminology

Why "PermissionSet" not "Role"? The name PermissionSet avoids collision with the existing RoleAssignment.role field. A group and its permission set share the same name by default (e.g. "Volunteer Coordinator"), but they diverge when an affiliate customizes — a group renamed to "Cabin Fairy" still points at the "Housing Coordinator" permission set underneath.

Questions for the Community
  • Should the migration preserve old RoleAssignment rows indefinitely or set a cleanup timeline?
  • Is mapping AFFILIATE_ADMIN to the "Affiliate Admin" group (oversight, restricted) correct, or should it map to Camp Director (operational)?
  • Should users with both AFFILIATE_ADMIN and AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER get both groups, or should we pick the higher-privilege one?
Security Considerations
  • Org isolation enforced at model level: GroupMembership → UserGroup → Affiliate → Organization
  • Permission validation: PermissionSet.permissions validated against the catalog (#301)
  • Migration is reversible for rollback safety
  • Central role escalation preserved — CENTRAL_SUPERADMIN passes all permission checks in same org
  • Old RoleAssignment rows preserved during migration, removed only after verification

Key Design Decisions

a. Restricted by default

Affiliates expand, not contract. The default template grants the minimum permissions needed for each role. The Team & Roles UI (#305) surfaces available permissions with descriptions, so affiliates can consciously add access where needed.

b. System permission sets can't be deleted

Default sets are marked is_system=True. Affiliates can modify permissions within a system set, but they cannot delete it. This prevents accidental removal of a foundational role that staff depend on.

c. Groups are renameable

"Housing Coordinator" can become "Cabin Fairy" if your affiliate calls it that. The permission set underneath stays the same — only the display name changes. This lets affiliates use their own terminology without losing the structured permission backing.

d. Camp Director only — no Asst. Director

There is one operational head role, not two. Affiliates that need an "Assistant Director" can clone the Camp Director permission set and trim permissions to create a custom role. This keeps the default template simple while allowing flexibility.

e. staff.manage on Camp Director only

Only Camp Director manages groups and memberships. Affiliate Admin is an oversight role, not an access-configuration role. This separation prevents an oversight-only admin from silently granting themselves or others elevated access.

f. Board governance excluded

No board permissions appear in this template. Board access is managed separately via the BoardMember model, which has its own governance lifecycle independent of the group/permission system.

Implementation

Provisioning happens via a Django signal on Affiliate creation, or by calling provision_affiliate_defaults(affiliate) directly. The default permission sets are defined in apps/tenancy/default_permission_sets.py as a data structure — a list of named sets, each with its permission list.

The provisioning function is idempotent: re-running it on an existing affiliate will not duplicate groups or permission sets. This means it can safely be called from a migration (see #302), from the signal, or manually if something went wrong during initial setup.

python 
# apps/tenancy/default_permission_sets.py
# Data structure defining the 8 default groups + permission sets

DEFAULT_GROUPS = [
    {
        'name': 'Affiliate Admin',
        'permissions': [
            'housing.read', 'volunteers.read',
            'registrations.read', 'sessions.read',
            'events.read', 'forms.read',
            'forms.submissions_view', 'medical.read',
            'reports.view_volunteer', 'reports.view_registration',
            'reports.view_financial', 'reports.view_bgc',
            'reports.view_attendance', 'reports.view_training',
            'community.post', 'library.read',
        ],
    },
    {
        'name': 'Camp Director',
        'permissions': ['*'],  # full operational access
    },
    # ... 6 more groups
]

def provision_affiliate_defaults(affiliate):
    """Create default groups + permission sets for an affiliate. Idempotent."""
    for group_def in DEFAULT_GROUPS:
        perm_set, created = PermissionSet.objects.get_or_create(
            organization=affiliate.organization,
            name=group_def['name'],
            defaults={'permissions': group_def['permissions'], 'is_system': True},
        )
        UserGroup.objects.get_or_create(
            affiliate=affiliate,
            name=group_def['name'],
            defaults={'permission_set': perm_set},
        )

Access Refactor

Replace role-string checks with permission-based checks across all views

#304 enhancement depends on: #301, #302, #303

The Problem

Currently access.py functions like user_has_affiliate_role(user, affiliate) check for role strings (AFFILIATE_ADMIN, AFFILIATE_VOLUNTEER_MANAGER). Decorators like @require_affiliate_role pass if the user holds any affiliate role. There is no way to gate a view on a specific capability (e.g., "can write housing") without hardcoding role checks directly in the view body — which scatters access logic across the codebase and makes it impossible to reason about who can do what from a single place.

New Access Functions

The refactor introduces permission-aware functions that check the UserGroup → PermissionSet → Permission chain instead of flat role strings. These become the new building blocks for all affiliate-scoped access decisions.

python 
def user_has_affiliate_permission(user, affiliate, permission: str) -> bool:
    '''True if the user holds the given permission on this affiliate
       (via any group membership), OR has a central role in the same org.'''
    ...

def user_has_any_affiliate_permission(user, affiliate, *permissions) -> bool:
    '''True if the user holds at least one of the given permissions.'''
    ...

And the new decorator:

python 
@require_affiliate_permission("housing.write")
def affiliate_cabin_assign(request, slug):
    ...

Decorator Migration Table

Every existing @require_affiliate_role decorator and the _require_affiliate_admin() guard need to be mapped to a specific permission. This is the full set of old-to-new mappings:

Old New
@require_affiliate_role (on volunteer views) @require_affiliate_permission("volunteers.read")
@require_affiliate_role (on housing views) @require_affiliate_permission("housing.write")
@require_affiliate_role (on report views) @require_affiliate_permission("reports.view_*") (per-report)
@require_affiliate_role (on session views) @require_affiliate_permission("sessions.write")
@require_affiliate_role (on event views) @require_affiliate_permission("events.write")
_require_affiliate_admin(request) (team management) @require_affiliate_permission("staff.manage")

    ...

Central Role Escalation

Central roles escalate into all affiliates

Central roles (CENTRAL_SUPERADMIN) continue to escalate into all affiliates within the same org. Permission check functions detect central RoleAssignment rows and grant all permissions — same as current behavior. No view-level special-casing is needed; the escalation happens inside user_has_affiliate_permission itself.

What Stays Unchanged

Not every access check changes. The following decorators and access models remain exactly as they are today — they are orthogonal to the affiliate permission model and operate on different relationships:

  • @require_central_role — central roles stay as RoleAssignment
  • @require_parent / @require_volunteer — end-user roles stay as RoleAssignment
  • @require_platform_admin — unchanged
  • Board decorators (@require_board_member, @require_board_chair_or_secretary) — unchanged, board has its own access model via BoardMember

Every view currently decorated with @require_affiliate_role needs to be audited and mapped to the correct permission. There are approximately 30+ affiliate-scoped views spread across the following files:

  • apps/web/views.py
  • apps/compliance/views.py

  • apps/camp/views.py
  • apps/events/views.py
  • apps/volunteers/views.py
  • apps/training/views.py

Each view must be examined, its domain and action determined, and the correct permission string applied. The migration table above is the starting point, but the audit is what confirms the mapping is right for every individual view.

Teams & Session Scoping

Issue #306 — Teams as membership containers: team model, group auto-grant, and session scoping

GitHub Issue

Summary

Teams are a first-class concept above UserGroup (#302). A Team is a named group of people that grants UserGroup memberships to its members. This provides two capabilities the base RBAC model cannot express:

  1. Bulk group assignment — add a user to a team, they get all groups linked to that team. Remove them, the groups go away (unless directly assigned).
  2. Session-scoped access — team memberships can be time-limited and session-scoped, so a cabin lead for Session A automatically loses access when Session A ends.

Sixth building block of the affiliate RBAC redesign (#300). Depends on #302 (models) and #304 (permission checks). The Teams UI tab in the #305 mockup is implemented by this issue.

Why Teams?

The UserGroup → PermissionSet → Permission model from #301-#305 only supports direct user-to-group assignments. Real camp operations have patterns that don't fit:

Scenario Today (without teams) With teams
Session cabin leads Give housing.write permanently, or don't give it at all Session-scoped team grants Housing Coordinator group; membership auto-expires
Staffing committee Each person individually added to the group Add everyone to the team once; team auto-grants the group
Multi-hat volunteers Multiple direct group assignments, manually tracked Direct group + team membership; effective permissions are the union
Medical team rotation Permanently assigned, manually removed later Session-scoped team; access auto-expires with the session

Data Model

Team

class Team(TimeStampedModel):
    name = models.CharField(max_length=100)
    affiliate = models.ForeignKey(Affiliate, on_delete=models.CASCADE, related_name="teams")
    description = models.TextField(blank=True)
    is_system = models.BooleanField(default=False)
    session = models.ForeignKey("apps.sessions.Session", null=True, blank=True, on_delete=models.CASCADE)
    expires_at = models.DateTimeField(null=True, blank=True)  # null = no expiration

    class Meta:
        constraints = [
            UniqueConstraint(fields=["name", "affiliate"], name="unique_team_name_per_affiliate"),
        ]
  • session is optional. If set, the team is session-scoped. If null, it's a standing team.
  • expires_at is optional. Can be set independently of session.
  • is_system prevents deletion (no system teams shipped by default, reserved for future).

TeamGroupLink

class TeamGroupLink(TimeStampedModel):
    team = models.ForeignKey(Team, on_delete=models.CASCADE, related_name="group_links")
    user_group = models.ForeignKey(UserGroup, on_delete=models.CASCADE, related_name="team_links")

    class Meta:
        constraints = [
            UniqueConstraint(fields=["team", "user_group"], name="unique_team_group_link"),
        ]

A team links to one or more UserGroups. When a user joins the team, they receive membership in all linked groups. When they leave, team-granted memberships are removed (unless the user also has a direct membership).

TeamMembership

class TeamMembership(TimeStampedModel):
    user = models.ForeignKey(settings.AUTH_USER_MODEL, on_delete=models.CASCADE)
    team = models.ForeignKey(Team, on_delete=models.CASCADE, related_name="memberships")
    is_lead = models.BooleanField(default=False)
    assigned_by = models.ForeignKey(settings.AUTH_USER_MODEL, null=True, blank=True, on_delete=models.SET_NULL)

    class Meta:
        constraints = [
            UniqueConstraint(fields=["user", "team"], name="unique_team_membership"),
        ]

is_lead grants the team lead the ability to manage their own team's membership (add/remove members) without needing staff.manage. This mirrors the mockup behavior where Sarah Chen manages the Housing Team because she's its lead.

Permission Resolution

The effective group set for a user becomes:

effective_groups(user, affiliate) = direct_groups(user, affiliate) ∪ team_granted_groups(user, affiliate)

Where team_granted_groups resolves: for each active TeamMembership (not expired), find the team's TeamGroupLinks, and collect the linked UserGroups.

The permission check (user_has_affiliate_permission from #304) is updated to include team-granted groups in its resolution path. This is a change to the access layer, not a new decorator.

Expiration Handling

  • expires_at timestamp — checked at query time. Expired memberships are filtered out. No celery task needed for correctness; a periodic cleanup task can remove expired rows for hygiene.
  • session FK — when a Session is closed or archived, its teams' memberships are effectively inactive: team.session_id is None OR team.session.status != 'CLOSED'.

Team Lead Permissions

Team leads (users with TeamMembership.is_lead=True) can:

  • Add and remove members from their own team
  • View their team's group links

Team leads cannot:

  • Edit the team's name, description, or group links (requires staff.manage)
  • Delete the team (requires staff.manage)
  • Create new teams (requires staff.manage)
  • Manage other teams (requires staff.manage)

This is a relationship-based check (TeamMembership.is_lead=True AND team == the_team_in_question), not a PermissionSet permission. This avoids needing a new catalog permission for what is fundamentally a scoped ownership check.

What This Issue Does NOT Cover

  • Cabin-level scoping — scoping access to specific cabins within a session. Requires a cabin FK on TeamMembership or a separate scoping model. Explicitly deferred — the permission catalog (#301) has no cabin-scoped permissions yet.
  • Team permission sets — teams grant groups, not permission sets directly.
  • Cross-affiliate teams — teams are scoped to a single affiliate. Cross-affiliate coordination would require a different model.
  • Team inheritance — no nesting of teams within teams.

Security Considerations
